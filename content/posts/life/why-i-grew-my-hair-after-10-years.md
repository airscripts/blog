## Introduction

Hey there!  
This is a post I wanted to write for a long time.

For almost ten years, I did not really _do_ my hair. I would just buzz it off and move on. It looked like a practical choice from the outside, maybe even a clean one. But looking back, it was not only that.

It was a way to avoid something.

Before I even reached my twenties, my physical structure changed a lot. My head shape changed, my face changed, and even if I never really lost my hair or my hairline, I felt like I had less density than before. To someone else, maybe that would have been a small thing. To me, it was not small at all.

Because the problem was never just hair.  
The problem was identity.

---

## Trying To Freeze Myself

When I used to buzz my hair off, I told myself it was better that way. Easier. Cleaner. Simpler.  
But the truth is that I was trying to freeze my appearance.

Bodybuilding had changed me a lot and probably much faster than many guys my age. My body, face, and general presence were not the same anymore. I did not feel like the version of myself I still had in mind. And instead of learning how to meet that new person with kindness, I tried to stop the process aesthetically.

Buzzing my hair became a mask.  
Or maybe better said: a way to flee from accepting that I was growing up.

That period was hard. I cried a lot. It felt terrible to lose something of your own and then look in the mirror without fully recognizing yourself. There is a very particular pain in feeling estranged from your own reflection. It makes you feel as if something has been taken from you, even when life is simply moving.

And sometimes that is exactly what hurts the most: not loss itself, but change.

---

## The Vulnerability I Was Trying To Hide

The strange thing is that I understood the contradiction quite early.

Buzzing my hair was supposed to protect me, but not long after, I realized it was doing something else too: it was exposing my vulnerability instead of hiding it.

That was a hard lesson.  
I thought I was covering the wound, but I was really just giving it another shape.

Still, time did something important for me. Over the years, even the buzz cut stopped being “the bad thing.” I accepted who I was while being exposed. It became something I could inhabit without fear. That did not happen overnight. It took emotional intelligence, deep introspection, and many silent conversations with myself.

And maybe that is one of the hardest parts of growing older: not changing, but learning how to remain present while you change.

---

## What Research Helped Me Understand

In the years that followed, I read a lot. About hair, baldness, aging, self-image, and the strange relationship we all have with our bodies.

The most important lesson I learned was also the simplest one: most people are too busy with their own lives to analyze us the way we analyze ourselves.

Psychology even has a name for this tendency: the **spotlight effect**. We often believe our flaws, insecurities, or changes are being observed and judged much more than they really are. In reality, other people are usually carrying their own worries, their own mirror, their own silent battles.

I wish I had known this from the beginning.  
It would have helped a lot.

Because what I called a “problem” was often loudest in my own head. Not always in reality.

Another thing I came to understand is that kindness toward yourself matters. Research around self-compassion and body image points in a hopeful direction: the more we learn to relate to ourselves with care instead of cruelty, the less trapped we tend to become in body-image suffering.

That does not mean “just don’t care.”
And it does not mean pain is fake.

It means pain can be met differently.

---

## What I Really Lost

If I have to name what I really lost back then, I would say: identity.  
That was the deepest wound.

Not because hair is everything, and not because appearance defines the soul, but because our physical self is still part of how we move through the world. When that changes before we are emotionally ready, it can feel like the map of ourselves has been redrawn without permission.

Later on, I learned something else too: the thing that truly matters most in the end is the inner self.

But I do not think that truth should become an excuse to abandon the body.

Sometimes people say things like: _I’m getting older, who cares about my skin, my hair, my appearance._

I understand the temptation in that sentence, but I do not agree with it.  
The spirit has the heaviest weight, yes.
But the body still deserves love.

You can care for your skin.
You can care for your hair.
You can fight for your body.
You can do all of it in love.

And you can do it at every age.

Loving yourself does not mean putting spirit above everything else and leaving the body in a dying place. It means embracing the whole person.

---

## Why I Grew My Hair

So why did I grow my hair after ten years?

Not because I suddenly became obsessed with appearance.
Not because I wanted to go backwards.
Not because I thought hair would solve something magical.

I grew my hair because I had healed enough to stop hiding from change.  
It was a psychological reclaiming before it was an aesthetic one.

I wanted to recover what I had lost internally years before: the ability to look at myself honestly, to stay there, and not panic.  
Growing my hair became a quiet act of happiness.

A way of saying:
I am here.
I see what changed.
I am not running anymore.

---

## Going To A Barber

The most surprising part of all this was what happened when I finally went to an actual barber.

I expected at least a little tension.
A little awkwardness.
Maybe a strange emotional weight.  
Instead, what I felt was peace.

I was happy to be there.
It felt natural.
It felt safe.  
And I think that says everything.

After so many years of internal noise, the real sign of healing was not drama. It was calm. It was sitting there and feeling that this did not have to be a battle anymore.

Soon I’ll have my second haircut too.

And maybe that is the most beautiful detail of all: what once felt emotionally charged has slowly become part of normal life again.

---

## Pain, Happiness, And Being Alive

If I could tell my younger self one thing, it would probably be this:  
Pain and suffering also exist to give respectable value to good and happiness.

I do not mean that suffering is romantic.
I do not mean that pain should be sought.
I only mean that once life wounds you, you begin to understand the weight of what is good in a different way.

Peace has a different taste after fear.
Happiness has a different light after grief.
Acceptance has a different dignity after shame.  
And maybe this is why I wanted to write this at all.

Because I know I am not the only one who has fought a silent and internal battle with himself.

Some people do it with hair.
Some with aging.
Some with their skin.
Some with weight.
Some with grief.
Some with things that have no visible name at all.

But the battle is real anyway.

---

## Conclusion

If there is one thing I want this post to give, it is courage.

Courage to all those people who are quietly fighting themselves while trying to stay functional, kind, and alive.

We are all weak in ways we do not always show.
And yet the truth is that it takes courage to be alive in the first place.

So if you are here, if you are still trying, if you are still learning how to look at yourself without fear, then you are already doing something brave.

That does not make you broken.
It makes you human.

And maybe that is what I finally learned through all of this: self-acceptance is not surrender, and caring for yourself is not vanity. You can love your inner world deeply and still fight for your body with tenderness. You can age and still care. You can change and still remain yourself.

You can love yourself at all the ages.
